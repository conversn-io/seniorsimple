What is Medicare Supplement Insurance?

Medicare Supplement Insurance, also known as Medigap, is private insurance designed to fill the "gaps" in Original Medicare coverage. These policies help pay for costs that Medicare doesn't cover, such as copayments, coinsurance, and deductibles.

Key Point: Medigap policies only work with Original Medicare (Parts A and B), not Medicare Advantage plans.

What Medigap Covers:

  • Medicare Part A coinsurance and hospital costs
  • Medicare Part B coinsurance or copayments
  • First 3 pints of blood each year
  • Part A hospice care coinsurance
  • Some foreign travel emergency care

Standardized Medigap Plans

Medigap policies are standardized by the federal government. This means Plan A offered by one insurance company has the same benefits as Plan A offered by another company. There are 10 standardized plans available in most states.

Enrollment Periods & Timing

Medigap Open Enrollment Period

Best Time to Enroll

This 6-month period begins the month you turn 65 AND are enrolled in Medicare Part B.

  • Guaranteed issue rights - cannot be denied coverage
  • Cannot be charged more due to health problems
  • Can buy any Medigap plan offered in your state

After Open Enrollment

Limited Rights

After your open enrollment period ends, you may face restrictions:

  • Insurance companies can deny coverage
  • Can charge more for health problems
  • Limited plan options may be available

Special Enrollment Situations

Trial Right

If you join a Medicare Advantage plan when first eligible for Medicare, you have a 12-month trial right to switch to Original Medicare and buy any Medigap plan.

Guaranteed Issue Rights

Certain situations give you guaranteed issue rights to buy a Medigap plan, such as losing employer coverage or your Medicare Advantage plan leaving the area.

Understanding Medigap Costs

Community-Rated

Everyone pays the same premium regardless of age. Premiums may increase due to inflation and other factors, but not because you get older.

Most Common

Issue-Age-Rated

Premium based on your age when you first buy the policy. Premiums don't increase as you get older, but may increase for other reasons.

Less Common

Attained-Age-Rated

Premiums increase as you get older. Usually has the lowest premium when you're younger but increases over time.

Least Favorable

How to Choose the Right Medigap Plan

Consider Your Priorities

Health Status

If you have chronic conditions or expect frequent healthcare needs, comprehensive coverage (Plan G) might be worth the higher premium.

Budget

Balance monthly premiums with potential out-of-pocket costs. Sometimes paying more monthly saves money annually.

Travel Plans

If you travel frequently or live in multiple states, consider plans with foreign travel emergency coverage.

Most Popular Choices

Plan G - Most Comprehensive

Best for those who want predictable costs and comprehensive coverage. Only requires paying the Part B deductible.

Good for: Frequent healthcare users, those wanting peace of mind

Plan N - Good Balance

Lower premium than Plan G but requires small copayments for office visits and ER visits.

Good for: Those comfortable with small copayments, moderate healthcare users

High-Deductible G - Budget Option

Much lower premium but high deductible ($2,800 in 2024). Good for those in excellent health.

Good for: Healthy individuals, those comfortable with higher out-of-pocket risk

Pro Tip: The "Plan G Strategy"

Many experts recommend Plan G as the "sweet spot" because it offers comprehensive coverage with just one predictable out-of-pocket cost (the Part B deductible). This makes budgeting for healthcare expenses much easier.

Common Medigap Mistakes to Avoid

Waiting Too Long to Enroll

Missing your 6-month open enrollment period can result in medical underwriting, higher premiums, or coverage denial.

Not Shopping Around

Premiums for identical plans can vary significantly between insurance companies. Always compare prices from multiple insurers.

Confusing Medigap with Drug Coverage

Medigap plans don't cover prescription drugs. You'll need separate Medicare Part D coverage for medications.

Focusing Only on Premium Cost

A lower premium might mean higher out-of-pocket costs when you need care. Consider total annual costs.

Not Verifying Agent Credentials

Work only with licensed insurance agents. Verify their credentials through your state's insurance department.

Not Reading the Fine Print

Understand exactly what's covered and what isn't. Pay attention to exclusions and limitations.

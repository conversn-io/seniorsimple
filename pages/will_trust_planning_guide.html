Will & Trust Planning Guide

Understand the differences and choose the right option for your family

Why Estate Planning Matters

Estate planning is one of the most important financial decisions you'll make, yet many people delay it or feel overwhelmed by the options. The two most common estate planning tools are wills and trusts, each serving different purposes and offering distinct advantages.

This comprehensive guide will help you understand the key differences between wills and trusts, when to use each, and how to make the right choice for your family's unique situation.

Key Takeaway

Most people need a will at minimum, but many benefit from having both a will and a trust. The right choice depends on your assets, family situation, and estate planning goals.

What is a Will?

A will is a legal document that specifies how your assets should be distributed after your death. It's the foundation of most estate plans and provides clear instructions for your executor to follow.

Key Components of a Will:

Executor Appointment

Names the person who will manage your estate

Beneficiary Designations

Specifies who receives your assets

Guardian Nominations

Names guardians for minor children

Asset Distribution

How property should be divided

Debt Instructions

How debts and taxes should be paid

Personal Wishes

Funeral arrangements and final requests

Advantages of Wills

  • Less expensive to create
  • Easy to update or change
  • Can name guardians for children
  • Simple to understand and execute
  • Legally recognized in all states

Limitations of Wills

  • Must go through probate court
  • Becomes public record
  • Probate process can take months
  • No protection if you become incapacitated
  • Probate costs can be significant

What is a Trust?

A trust is a legal arrangement where you transfer ownership of assets to a trustee who manages them according to your instructions for the benefit of your chosen beneficiaries.

Key Players in a Trust:

Grantor/Settlor

The person who creates and funds the trust

Trustee

The person or entity that manages the trust

Beneficiaries

The people who receive benefits from the trust

Types of Trusts:

Revocable Living Trust

Can be changed or cancelled during your lifetime. You typically serve as trustee while alive and competent.

Irrevocable Trust

Cannot be easily changed once created. Offers tax benefits and asset protection but less flexibility.

Testamentary Trust

Created through your will and only takes effect after death. Must go through probate.

Advantages of Trusts

  • Avoids probate court
  • Maintains privacy
  • Faster asset distribution
  • Incapacity protection
  • Greater control over distributions

Disadvantages of Trusts

  • Higher upfront costs
  • More complex to set up
  • Requires ongoing maintenance
  • Must transfer assets to trust
  • Cannot name guardians for children

When to Choose Each Option

Choose a Will When:

  • Your estate is valued under $100,000
  • You have minor children (need guardian nomination)
  • Simple asset distribution is sufficient
  • Budget is a primary concern
  • You don't own property in multiple states
  • Privacy is not a major concern
  • You want maximum flexibility to make changes
  • Your family situation is straightforward

Choose a Trust When:

  • Your estate is valued over $100,000
  • You want to avoid probate court
  • Privacy is important to you
  • You want incapacity protection
  • You own property in multiple states
  • You have complex family situations
  • You want to control how assets are distributed
  • You have beneficiaries who need protection

The Best of Both Worlds: Will + Trust

Why Many People Choose Both

Many estate planning attorneys recommend having both a will and a trust. This combination provides comprehensive coverage for all your estate planning needs.

How They Work Together:

Trust Handles:

  • • Major assets (home, investments, accounts)
  • • Incapacity protection
  • • Probate avoidance
  • • Privacy protection
  • • Controlled distributions

Will Handles:

  • • Guardian nominations for minor children
  • • Assets not transferred to trust
  • • Personal property and sentimental items
  • • Funeral arrangements
  • • Final wishes and instructions

Pour-Over Will

When you have a trust, you'll typically also have a "pour-over will" that transfers any assets not already in the trust into the trust upon your death. This ensures nothing is forgotten.

Implementation Steps

Step 1: Assess Your Situation

  • • Calculate your total estate value
  • • List all assets and their ownership
  • • Identify your beneficiaries
  • • Consider your family dynamics
  • • Evaluate your privacy needs
  • • Assess incapacity concerns
  • • Review your budget for costs
  • • Consider your state's probate laws

Step 2: Choose Your Professional Team

  • • Estate planning attorney
  • • Financial advisor
  • • Tax professional (CPA)
  • • Insurance agent
  • • Get referrals from trusted sources
  • • Check credentials and experience
  • • Interview multiple professionals
  • • Understand fee structures

Step 3: Draft Your Documents

Work with your attorney to draft the appropriate documents based on your chosen strategy:

  • • Will (simple or complex)
  • • Trust agreement (if applicable)
  • • Power of attorney documents
  • • Healthcare directives

Step 4: Execute and Fund

Properly execute your documents and transfer assets:

  • • Sign documents with proper witnesses
  • • Transfer assets to trust (if applicable)
  • • Update beneficiary designations
  • • Notify relevant parties

Step 5: Maintain and Update

Keep your estate plan current:

  • • Review annually or after major life events
  • • Update beneficiary information
  • • Transfer new assets to trust
  • • Adjust for law changes

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Procrastination

Many people delay estate planning, thinking they have plenty of time. Unexpected events can happen at any age, leaving your family without proper guidance.

Forgetting to Fund the Trust

Creating a trust but failing to transfer assets into it renders the trust useless. This is one of the most common and costly mistakes.

Outdated Beneficiary Designations

Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance policies override your will or trust. Keep these updated.

DIY Estate Planning

While online forms exist, estate planning involves complex legal and tax issues. Professional guidance is crucial for proper implementation.

Ignoring State Law Differences

Estate laws vary significantly by state. Moving to a new state may require updating your estate plan to comply with local laws.

Final Thoughts

The decision between a will and a trust—or using both—depends on your unique circumstances, family situation, and financial goals. There's no one-size-fits-all answer, but understanding the differences helps you make an informed choice.

Remember that estate planning is not a one-time event. Your plan should evolve as your life changes, your family grows, and your assets accumulate. Regular reviews with your estate planning attorney ensure your plan remains effective and current.

The most important step is to start. Even a simple will is better than no estate plan at all. Your family will thank you for taking the time to plan ahead and provide clear guidance for your legacy.

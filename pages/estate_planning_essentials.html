Your 7-Step Estate Planning Journey

1

Take Inventory of Your Assets

Before you can plan your estate, you need to know what you have. This includes not just your bank accounts and investments, but also your home, personal property, life insurance policies, and retirement accounts.

Financial Assets

  • Bank accounts (checking, savings, CDs)
  • Investment accounts (stocks, bonds, mutual funds)
  • Retirement accounts (401k, IRA, pension)
  • Life insurance policies
  • Annuities

Physical Assets

  • Primary residence
  • Vacation homes or rental properties
  • Vehicles
  • Valuable personal items (jewelry, art, collectibles)
  • Business interests
2

Calculate Your Estate's Value

Understanding your estate's total value is crucial for tax planning and determining which estate planning tools you'll need. Don't forget to subtract any debts, mortgages, or other liabilities.

Important: For 2024, the federal estate tax exemption is $13.61 million per person ($27.22 million for married couples). However, this exemption is set to decrease significantly in 2026 unless Congress acts.

3

Create Your Will

Your will is the foundation of your estate plan. It's a legal document that specifies how you want your assets distributed after your death and who will be responsible for carrying out your wishes.

Executor

Choose someone you trust to manage your estate

Beneficiaries

Specify who receives your assets

Guardian

Name a guardian for minor children

Warning: A will alone may not be sufficient for larger estates or complex family situations. It also doesn't avoid probate court.

4

Establish Power of Attorney Documents

Power of Attorney documents allow someone you trust to make decisions on your behalf if you become incapacitated. These are just as important as your will, but they take effect while you're still alive.

Financial Power of Attorney

Allows someone to handle your financial affairs, including:

  • Pay bills and manage accounts
  • Make investment decisions
  • File tax returns
  • Manage real estate

Healthcare Power of Attorney

Allows someone to make medical decisions, including:

  • Choose doctors and treatments
  • Access medical records
  • Make end-of-life decisions
  • Authorize or refuse treatment
5

Consider Trust Structures

Trusts can provide significant benefits, including avoiding probate, reducing taxes, and protecting assets from creditors. Here are the most common types to consider:

Revocable Living Trust

The most popular trust for estate planning. You maintain control during your lifetime, and assets pass to beneficiaries without probate.

Advantages:
  • Avoids probate
  • Maintains privacy
  • Flexible and changeable
Disadvantages:
  • No tax benefits
  • Requires asset retitling
  • More complex than a will

Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

Removes life insurance from your taxable estate while providing liquidity to pay estate taxes.

Best for: Estates over the federal exemption limit with significant life insurance policies.

Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

Provides income during your lifetime, with remainder going to charity. Offers tax deductions and reduces estate taxes.

Best for: Those with charitable intentions and appreciated assets.

6

Plan for Tax Efficiency

Estate taxes can significantly reduce what your heirs receive. Here are key strategies to minimize the tax burden:

Annual Gifting

You can gift up to $18,000 per person per year (2024 limit) without tax consequences.

Example: A married couple can gift $36,000 per year to each child, removing $180,000 from their estate over 5 years to 5 children.

Charitable Giving

Donations to qualified charities are deductible from your taxable estate.

  • Direct charitable bequests
  • Charitable remainder trusts
  • Charitable lead trusts
7

Review and Update Regularly

Your estate plan isn't a "set it and forget it" document. Life changes, and your estate plan should change with it. Review your plan whenever you experience major life events.

When to Review Your Estate Plan:

  • Marriage or divorce
  • Birth or adoption of children
  • Death of a beneficiary
  • Significant change in assets
  • Move to a different state
  • Changes in tax laws
  • Retirement
  • Every 3-5 years minimum

Common Estate Planning Mistakes to Avoid

Procrastination

Waiting until it's too late. Start planning now, regardless of your age or wealth.

Forgetting to Update Beneficiaries

Beneficiary designations override your will. Update them after major life changes.

Not Funding Your Trust

A trust is only effective if you transfer assets into it. Don't forget this crucial step.

Choosing the Wrong Executor

Your executor should be trustworthy, organized, and capable of handling financial matters.

DIY Complex Situations

While simple wills can be done yourself, complex estates need professional help.

Ignoring Tax Implications

Consider the tax consequences of your estate plan for both you and your beneficiaries.

When to Seek Professional Help

While some estate planning can be done yourself, certain situations require professional expertise. Consider hiring an estate planning attorney if:

Complex Assets

  • Business ownership
  • Multiple properties
  • Significant investments
  • Valuable collections

Family Situations

  • Blended families
  • Minor children
  • Special needs dependents
  • Estranged family members

Tax Concerns

  • Estate over $13M
  • Foreign assets
  • Large gifts planned
  • Charitable giving goals
